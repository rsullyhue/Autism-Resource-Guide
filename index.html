A Comprehensive Guide for Educators in Concord, CA & Contra Costa County

Academic Supports Transition Services Trauma-Informed Practices Local Schools & Programs About

Welcome!

This resource guide was created for educators working with autistic students in K-12 settings, particularly in the Concord, CA and Contra Costa County area. Whether you're looking for evidence-based academic interventions, transition support, trauma-informed practices, or local service providers, you'll find vetted, practical resources here.

All resources listed are free or publicly accessible unless otherwise noted. Resources are organized by category for easy navigation.

üìö Academic Supports & Evidence-Based Practices

AFIRM (Autism Focused Intervention Resources & Modules) Online

Free evidence-based practices for teaching autistic students.

Why I include this: AFIRM has been a lifesaver for me as a newer special ed teacher. It's not just theory, they actually show you step-by-step how to implement each practice with real classroom examples. I've used their visual support module and prompting strategies guide when writing my lesson plans this year. The video examples are especially helpful because you can see what it actually looks like in a classroom, not just read about it.
What you need to know: Run by UNC Chapel Hill (Frank Porter Graham Institute) with DOE funding. They review research to identify which practices actually work, so all 27 practices on there have solid evidence behind them.
What's on there:
  • Implementation guides for 27 evidence-based practices
  • Checklists you can actually use to make sure you're doing it right
  • Video examples (these are really helpful)
  • Case studies
  • Self-paced PD modules
IRIS Center, Autism Modules Online

Professional development modules for teachers.

Why it's useful: Perfect for when you need PD hours or want to learn about something specific quickly. I used their modules when I was trying to figure out better ways to accommodate reading for some of my students. The scenarios feel realistic and you can work through them at your own pace. Plus you get a certificate at the end which is nice for your file.
What's on there:
  • Interactive modules with quizzes
  • Accommodation checklists (I've printed several of these)
  • Video examples
  • Resources on behavior support, assistive tech, IEP development
  • Certificates for PD credit
It's from Vanderbilt and funded by the Department of Education, so it's legit research-based stuff.
Understood.org Online

Parent and educator resources on learning differences and disabilities.

Why I recommend it: Good for teachers but especially helpful to share with families who need plain-language explanations of stuff. I've sent parents here when they're trying to understand their child's IEP or want ideas for home. The IEP goal bank is something I use when I'm writing goals, it helps me make sure my language is clear and measurable.
What it has:
  • IEP goal bank (this is really helpful)
  • Articles on accommodations and modifications
  • Parent-friendly explanations
  • Advocacy tips
It's a nonprofit, so no ads or weird sales pitches. Everything is written to be accessible.
Organization for Autism Research (OAR) Online

Applied research and practical tools for autism.

What it is: I haven't used this one as much as AFIRM, but their "Life Journey Through Autism" guidebooks are really comprehensive. They cover different life stages and are written for families and educators. If you're working with older students and thinking about transition, their guides on adult life are solid.
What you need to know: They fund applied research (research that's meant to be useful in real life, not just theoretical). Their guidebooks synthesize research into practical advice.
Contra Costa SELPA Local

Special Education Local Plan Area for Contra Costa County.

Why it matters: This is our county's SELPA, they coordinate special ed services across all the districts in Contra Costa. They run professional development workshops that are free for teachers in the county. I went to one on AAC implementation last year that was really practical. They also have resources on compliance stuff (assessments, IEP procedures) which is good to know when you're newer to special ed.
What they offer:
  • Free PD workshops for county educators
  • Technical assistance
  • Special ed compliance resources
  • Low-incidence materials (for kids with significant disabilities)
They're required by California Ed Code, so they're the official entity for special ed coordination in our county.
Mount Diablo Unified School District (MDUSD) Special Education Local

Local district special education services.

What you need to know: My district! This is where I work, so I'm obviously biased, but MDUSD offers the full range of special ed services from inclusion support to specialized SDC classes (like mine). If you're in the area and looking for placement options or want to know what services are available locally, this is where to start.
Services available:
  • Full continuum of services (inclusion to SDC)
  • Related services (speech, OT, APE, etc.)
  • Transition program for 18-22 year olds
  • Assistive technology support
We follow IDEA requirements like all public schools in California.

üéì Transition Services & Post-Secondary Support

NTACT (National Technical Assistance Center on Transition) Online

Evidence-based practices for transition planning.

Why it's useful: When you're writing transition goals for IEPs or trying to figure out what actually helps students after high school, this is the place. They have something called the "Predictors" which are research-backed factors that lead to better post-school outcomes. I reference these when I'm justifying why we're focusing on certain transition activities.
What you need to know: Funded by the Department of Education and run by universities. Their "evidence-based practices" and "predictors" are based on large studies tracking what actually helps students succeed after high school.
What's available:
  • Predictors of Post-School Success
  • Effective Practices database
  • Transition assessment tools
  • Sample IEP transition goals
Autism Speaks, Employment Toolkit Online

Resources for employment preparation and job search.

Full disclosure: Autism Speaks is controversial in the autistic community because of their history (they used to push cure narratives and didn't include autistic people in leadership). That said, I've been told that their employment toolkit is actually pretty practical. Just wanted to be upfront about the controversy. If you use their stuff, I'd recommend also looking at ASAN's resources to get the autistic perspective.
What's in the toolkit:
  • Job search strategies
  • Interview prep
  • Disclosure guidance (whether to tell employers)
  • Workplace accommodations
Think College Online

College programs for students with intellectual disabilities.

What it is: A searchable database of college programs for students with intellectual disabilities. If you have students who want to go to college but need more support than typical disability services provide, this is where to look. There are over 300 programs across the country now. Some are certificate programs, some are fully inclusive.
What you'll find:
  • Searchable database of 300+ programs
  • Program comparison tools
  • Research on postsecondary outcomes
  • Family guides
It's a project of the Institute for Community Inclusion at UMass Boston, funded by federal grants.
Transition Coalition Online

Transition assessment and planning tools.

Why I use it: They have a bunch of free assessment tools for transition planning, person-centered planning activities, and resources on self-determination. The person-centered planning stuff is especially good if you're trying to make IEP meetings less school-driven and more focused on what the student actually wants.
Available resources:
  • Free transition assessments
  • Person-centered planning materials
  • Self-determination resources
  • Sample forms and checklists
Run by the University of Kansas with federal funding.
Regional Center of the East Bay (RCEB) Local

Lifelong services for individuals with developmental disabilities.

This is huge: RCEB funds services that schools don't cover, respite for families, behavioral services outside school, day programs, supported living. As students get closer to transition age (18-22), RCEB becomes critical for setting up adult services. I've learned to start connecting families early because the earlier you build that relationship with a case manager, the smoother transition goes.
What you need to know: Regional centers are mandated by the Lanterman Act (California law). RCEB serves Alameda and Contra Costa counties. They develop an Individual Program Plan (IPP) which is like an IEP but for life, not just school.
What they provide:
  • Case management
  • Respite care
  • Supported employment
  • Day programs
  • Residential services
  • Behavior support
Department of Rehabilitation (DOR), Contra Costa Local

Vocational rehabilitation and employment services.

What it does: DOR helps people with disabilities get jobs. They can pay for job coaching, vocational training, even college if it's related to an employment goal. Students can apply while still in high school (usually junior or senior year), which gives you time to coordinate between school and DOR for transition.
Services include:
  • Vocational counseling and assessment
  • Job placement and coaching
  • Training and education funding
  • Assistive technology for employment
They're a state agency, services are free for eligible individuals. Funded through federal Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA).
Diablo Valley College, DSPS Local

College disability services for local community college.

Why I include this: DVC is the local community college and a lot of my students might go there after high school. Their DSPS (Disabled Students Programs and Services) provides accommodations in college, it's similar to what we do in K-12 but different rules since it's college. I send families here to tour and learn about the process before graduation so they know what to expect.
What DSPS offers:
  • Educational accommodations
  • Assistive technology
  • Counseling and educational planning
  • Learning disability assessment
  • Test proctoring
Community colleges in California are required to have DSPS under Section 504 and ADA.

National Child Traumatic Stress Network (NCTSN) Online

Resources for understanding and addressing childhood trauma.

Why this matters: Many students have experienced trauma, bullying, restraint/seclusion, medical procedures, other stuff. NCTSN helps you understand how trauma affects learning and behavior, which is critical because trauma responses can look like defiance or behavior problems when they're really not. Their school-specific resources help create classrooms where traumatized students feel safe enough to actually learn.
What you need to know: NCTSN is funded by SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration, a federal agency). It's a network of over 150 trauma centers that contribute to developing and sharing evidence-based practices.
Resources available:
  • Trauma screening and assessment tools
  • School-based interventions
  • Understanding trauma's impact on learning
  • Cultural considerations in trauma work
  • Resources for specific trauma types (abuse, medical, disasters)
SAMHSA Trauma Resources Online

Federal resources on trauma-informed care.

What it is: This is the federal government's main resource on trauma. They developed the six principles of trauma-informed care (safety, trustworthiness, peer support, collaboration, empowerment, cultural considerations) that everyone references. Good for understanding the big picture framework.
Key resources:
  • Six principles of trauma-informed care
  • Training materials
  • Implementation guides
  • Resources on specific populations
SAMHSA is part of the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, so this is official federal guidance.
Autistic Self Advocacy Network (ASAN) Online

Advocacy and resources by and for autistic people.

Why this is important: ASAN is run by and for autistic people, which is a perspective that's often missing from other resources. They explain why certain common practices (restraint, forced eye contact, compliance-based stuff) are traumatizing to autistic people. This has really changed how I think about some "standard" classroom management approaches. Their resources on communication access and self-advocacy are also really valuable for helping students understand and advocate for themselves.
What you need to know: ASAN is autistic-led (founded by Ari Ne'eman and other autistic self-advocates). Their perspective comes from lived experience rather than clinical/research perspectives. They've been influential in disability rights policy.
Key resources:
  • Policy papers on restraint/seclusion
  • Communication access materials
  • Self-advocacy toolkits
  • Critiques of ABA and compliance-based approaches
  • Resources for autistic adults
Zones of Regulation Online

Framework for teaching self-regulation and emotional control.

Why I use it: Zones gives students and teachers a shared way to talk about emotional states without judgment. The color-coded framework (blue, green, yellow, red) makes it accessible for students who struggle with abstract emotion words. I use this in my classroom and like that it acknowledges all emotions are valid, it's about learning what helps you regulate, not just "being good."
What you need to know: Developed by Leah Kuypers, an occupational therapist. It combines principles from cognitive-behavioral therapy, self-regulation theory, and social thinking. Widely used in schools, there's research supporting it but it's not as extensively studied as some other interventions.
Components:
  • Color-coded emotional framework
  • Sensory supports for regulation
  • Strategies for different zones
  • Curricula and lesson plans
Contra Costa Behavioral Health Services Local

County mental health services and crisis support.

What it provides: This is where you go when students have mental health needs schools can't handle alone, anxiety, depression, trauma. They can place therapists directly in schools or provide clinic services. They have 24/7 crisis support, which is important to know about. I've referred families here several times.
Services available:
  • School-based therapy
  • Clinic-based services
  • Crisis intervention (24/7)
  • Medication support
  • Case management
Part of Contra Costa County Health Services, serves all county residents. Services are often free or low-cost based on income.
NAMI (National Alliance on Mental Illness) Online

Mental health education, support, and advocacy.

Good resource for families: NAMI does family education programs and support groups. When families are dealing with mental health challenges alongside autism, NAMI can provide education about mental health conditions and connect them with other families going through similar stuff. They have a local Contra Costa chapter.
What they offer:
  • Family education programs
  • Support groups
  • Resources on specific mental health conditions
  • Advocacy training
NAMI is a grassroots organization with local chapters nationwide. Programs are typically free.
Seneca Family of Agencies Local

Intensive school-based mental health services.

What it is: Seneca provides intensive mental health services in schools (more intensive than typical school counseling). They work with students who have significant behavioral or emotional challenges. I've worked with their therapists in my classroom before, they do individual therapy but also help develop behavior plans and train staff.
Services:
  • School-based individual therapy
  • Group therapy
  • Crisis intervention
  • Behavioral consultation
  • Family support
Nonprofit agency providing evidence-based interventions. Services are typically funded through Medi-Cal or county mental health.

üè´ Local Schools & Specialized Programs

ACE Charter Schools (formerly Anova) Local

K-12 school specifically for autistic students.

What makes it unique: ACE is the only K-12 school in Contra Costa County that's specifically designed for autistic students. They use the Autism Model of Education (AME) which focuses on strengths-based, individualized instruction. Small class sizes, structured environment, focus on communication and social skills. Some of my students have gone there when they needed more specialized support than MDUSD could provide.
What you need to know: ACE is a nonpublic school (NPS) certified by the state. Students are usually placed there by their district through the IEP process when the district determines they need a more specialized setting. The district pays tuition.
Program features:
  • Small class sizes (typically 6-8 students per class)
  • Autism Model of Education (AME) approach
  • Speech, OT, counseling on-site
  • Structured, predictable environment
  • Functional life skills focus for older students
Orion Academy Local

College-prep school for students with autism and learning differences.

What it is: Orion is for students who are academically capable but need support with executive functioning, social skills, and organization. It's a college-prep program, students there are working on UC a-g requirements. Different vibe from ACE, Orion students are working at or near grade level academically but need the smaller class sizes and explicit social skills instruction.
What you need to know: Orion is also a nonpublic school (NPS). Their curriculum includes Social Thinking methodology (Michelle Garcia Winner) and they have UC-approved courses. Students can earn a regular high school diploma.
Program features:
  • College-prep academic program
  • Social Thinking curriculum
  • Executive function support
  • Small classes (8-12 students)
  • Transition support for college/career

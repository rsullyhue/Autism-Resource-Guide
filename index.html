AFIRM

Autism Focused Intervention Resources & Modules - Free evidence-based practices for teaching autistic students.

Why it's useful: AFIRM breaks down complex evidence-based practices into step-by-step implementation guides that teachers can actually use in their classrooms. Instead of just telling you what works, it shows you exactly how to do it with real examples. This is especially helpful for teachers who are new to working with autistic students or need to implement a specific practice they haven't used before.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: AFIRM is developed by the Frank Porter Graham Child Development Institute at UNC Chapel Hill and funded by the U.S. Department of Education's Office of Special Education Programs. All 27 practices included are identified as evidence-based through rigorous research reviews conducted by the National Professional Development Center on Autism Spectrum Disorder. Each practice has been validated through multiple peer-reviewed studies showing positive outcomes for autistic learners.
Specific features/tools available:
  • 27 evidence-based practices with complete implementation guides
  • Downloadable briefs explaining each practice in plain language
  • Implementation checklists to track fidelity
  • Video examples showing practices in real classroom settings
  • Case studies demonstrating how to apply practices to different students
  • Professional development modules for self-paced learning
  • Fidelity tools to assess how well you're implementing each practice

NTACT

National Technical Assistance Center on Transition - Premier resource for secondary transition.

Why it's useful: NTACT is the go-to resource for everything related to secondary transition. Starting at age 16 (or earlier in California), you're required to have transition planning in IEPs, and NTACT gives you the tools to do this well. They provide practical assessment tools to figure out what students actually need, not just generic transition goals. This is especially valuable when you're trying to write meaningful, individualized transition plans.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: NTACT is funded by the U.S. Department of Education's Office of Special Education Programs and operated by leading universities specializing in transition research. All resources are based on Predictor studies showing which factors lead to positive post-school outcomes for students with disabilities. Tools and strategies are vetted through peer review and tested in real schools before being disseminated. NTACT synthesizes decades of transition research into practical applications.
Specific features/tools available:
  • Age-appropriate transition assessments (interest inventories, career exploration tools)
  • Evidence-based predictor practices that improve post-school outcomes
  • Post-secondary education planning guides for college, vocational training, and adult education
  • Employment preparation curricula and job exploration activities
  • Independent living skills assessments and teaching resources
  • Self-determination curriculum and tools
  • Family engagement strategies for transition planning
  • IEP transition goals examples aligned with student assessments
  • Interagency collaboration frameworks

National Child Traumatic Stress Network

Comprehensive trauma-informed care resources for schools and educators.

Why it's useful: Many autistic students have experienced trauma, whether from bullying, restraint and seclusion, medical procedures, or other adverse experiences. NCTSN helps you understand how trauma affects learning and behavior, which is critical because trauma responses can look like behavior problems or non-compliance. Their school-specific resources help you create classrooms where traumatized students feel safe enough to learn.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: NCTSN is funded by the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) and includes more than 150 member centers across the U.S. All resources are based on research about childhood trauma and evidence-based trauma treatments. Materials are developed by licensed mental health professionals specializing in childhood trauma and reviewed by experts in trauma-informed care. The network has been the leading authority on child traumatic stress since 2001.
Specific features/tools available:
  • Trauma-informed school assessment and implementation tools
  • Creating safe and supportive classrooms guidelines
  • Understanding how trauma affects learning and behavior
  • Discipline approaches that don't re-traumatize
  • Crisis response planning for schools
  • Teacher self-care strategies and secondary trauma prevention
  • Trauma screening tools for schools
  • Cultural considerations in trauma-informed care
  • Helping traumatized children learn (specific classroom strategies)

  • Training curricula for school staff

SAMHSA Trauma Resources

Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration trauma-informed approach resources.

Why it's useful: SAMHSA provides the foundational framework for trauma-informed care that many school districts are now required to implement. Understanding their six key principles (safety, trustworthiness, peer support, collaboration, empowerment, cultural sensitivity) gives you a lens for evaluating all your classroom practices. Their resources help you shift from "What's wrong with this student?" to "What happened to this student?"
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: SAMHSA is a federal agency within the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. They fund trauma research and evidence-based treatment programs across the country. The trauma-informed approach framework is based on extensive research about adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) and their impact on development, learning, and mental health. SAMHSA's guidelines are used by schools, hospitals, and social service agencies nationwide as the standard for trauma-informed care.
Specific features/tools available:
  • Six key principles of trauma-informed approach
  • Implementation guide for schools and educational settings
  • School-based intervention strategies
  • Crisis response and de-escalation guidelines
  • Community trauma resources toolkit
  • Cultural competence in trauma-informed care
  • Training materials for staff development
  • Organizational self-assessment tools
  • Trauma screening and assessment guidance
  • Policy development resources for trauma-informed schools
  • Professional development modules
  • Family engagement strategies in trauma-informed systems

Autistic Self Advocacy Network

Autistic-led organization providing resources on supporting autistic individuals' mental health.

Why it's useful: ASAN provides the autistic perspective on trauma, which is often missing from other resources. They explain why certain common practices (restraint, forced eye contact, compliance-based interventions) are traumatizing to autistic people. This helps you avoid re-traumatizing students and understand why some students have strong negative reactions to things that seem harmless. Their communication strategies respect autistic ways of communicating rather than forcing neurotypical standards.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: ASAN is the leading autistic-led advocacy organization in the United States, run by and for autistic people. Their resources are grounded in the lived experiences of autistic individuals and informed by research on autism, trauma, and mental health. While they don't conduct traditional research studies, they provide crucial first-person perspectives that research often misses. Their materials are widely respected in the autism community and increasingly used by professionals who want to provide affirming, non-traumatizing support.
Specific features/tools available:
  • Understanding autistic trauma responses and how they differ from non-autistic trauma
  • Communication strategies that respect autistic communication styles

  • Alternatives to restraint and seclusion

Zones of Regulation

Framework for teaching self-regulation and emotional control, particularly helpful for trauma-affected students.

Why it's useful: Zones gives students (and teachers) a common language for talking about emotional states without judgment. The visual, color-coded framework makes it accessible for students who struggle with abstract emotion words. It's particularly helpful for autistic students because it acknowledges that all emotions are valid and focuses on learning what helps you regulate rather than just "being good." The framework includes sensory strategies that many autistic students find helpful.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: Zones of Regulation was developed by Leah Kuypers, an occupational therapist with extensive experience working with students with social-emotional and sensory needs. The curriculum integrates research on self-regulation, executive functions, and sensory processing. While the Zones framework itself is a specific program, it's based on well-established principles from cognitive behavioral therapy, sensory integration theory, and social thinking research. The program has been widely adopted and has case studies showing positive outcomes.
Specific features/tools available:
  • Visual framework with four color-coded zones (blue, green, yellow, red)
  • Emotion identification activities
  • Sensory strategies organized by zone
  • Cognitive strategies for self-regulation
  • Co-regulation techniques for adults
  • Tools for identifying triggers
  • Classroom implementation guides
  • Posters and visual supports ready to print
  • Size of the problem curriculum integration
  • Activities for teaching expected vs. unexpected behaviors
  • Data collection tools
  • Parent communication materials
  • Lesson plans for teaching zones concepts

ACE Contra Costa Campus (Anova)

Only K-12 school in Contra Costa County specializing in education for children with Autism Spectrum Disorders.

Why it's useful: ACE is the only school in Contra Costa County specifically designed for students with autism, using a comprehensive model that integrates education with therapy throughout the school day. This is particularly valuable for students who need intensive, specialized instruction and aren't making progress in traditional special education settings. The small class sizes and high staff-to-student ratio allow for highly individualized programming that's hard to achieve in public school settings.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: ACE uses the Anova Method of Education (AME), which integrates Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA), speech therapy, occupational therapy, and academics in an evidence-based framework. All teachers are credentialed special education teachers, and therapy services are provided by licensed clinicians (BCBAs, SLPs, OTs). The school follows California state standards and curriculum frameworks. ACE is a certified nonpublic school (NPS) that undergoes regular reviews by the California Department of Education to maintain certification.
Specific features/tools available:
  • K-12 education specifically for students with autism
  • Small class sizes (typically 6-8 students per class)
  • Credentialed special education teachers
  • Embedded ABA services throughout the school day
  • Speech therapy, occupational therapy, and adaptive PE integrated into classroom
  • California state standards-based curriculum adapted for autism
  • Structured teaching using visual supports and schedules
  • Social skills instruction and practice
  • Transition planning and life skills training for older students
  • Parent training and collaboration
  • Comprehensive assessments and progress monitoring
  • Specialized crisis intervention and behavior support

Orion Academy

First high school in California providing college-prep education specifically for students with autism spectrum disorder.

Why it's useful: Orion is one of the first schools in California designed specifically for college-bound autistic students who have the academic skills for college prep but struggle with the social and executive function demands of traditional high schools. This fills a gap for students who don't need academic modifications but do need support with organization, social interaction, and managing the hidden curriculum of school. The school explicitly teaches the social and pragmatic skills that are assumed in typical schools.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: Orion Academy is a certified nonpublic school that meets California Department of Education standards. The curriculum uses evidence-based approaches for teaching social cognition (including Social Thinking curriculum and executive function strategies). All courses are UC-approved college prep classes, meeting rigorous academic standards. Teachers have credentials in their subject areas plus training in autism and social-pragmatic instruction. The school's model is based on research about what helps autistic students succeed academically while addressing their social-cognitive differences.
Specific features/tools available:
  • Grades 8-12 college preparatory curriculum
  • All courses UC a-g approved for college admission
  • Small class sizes (8-12 students)
  • Explicit instruction in social cognition and pragmatic language
  • Executive function support and organizational strategies
  • Supported transitions between classes and activities
  • Lunch bunch and structured social opportunities
  • College counseling and application support
  • SAT/ACT preparation
  • Self-advocacy skills development
  • Internship and community experience opportunities
  • Parent workshops on supporting teenagers with autism
  • Collaboration with therapists and outside providers
  • Modified schedule options for students who need them

Mount Diablo Unified School District

Your local public school district serving Concord with special education programs and services.

Why it's useful: MDUSD is your local public school district, making it the most accessible option for most families in Concord. The district offers a range of special education services from full inclusion with support to specialized special day classes. Understanding what MDUSD offers helps you advise families appropriately and connect students with services. For teachers working in MDUSD, this is where you'll access support, resources, and collaboration with other special educators.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: As a public school district, MDUSD must comply with federal IDEA requirements and California Education Code standards for special education. Services are provided by credentialed teachers and licensed therapists. The district is required to provide FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education) based on individual student needs, using evidence-based practices. MDUSD receives oversight from Contra Costa SELPA and the California Department of Education, with regular program reviews and monitoring for compliance and outcomes.
Specific features/tools available:
  • Special Day Classes (SDC) for students with moderate to severe autism
  • Inclusion support models (push-in services, co-teaching)
  • Full continuum of placement options
  • Speech and language therapy
  • Occupational therapy services
  • Adapted physical education
  • Behavior intervention specialists and support
  • Assistive technology assessments and lending library
  • Augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) support
  • Transition program for students ages 18-22 focusing on employment and independence
  • Preschool special education programs
  • Professional development for general and special education staff
  • Parent education workshops and support groups
  • Access to resource specialists and program specialists

Regional Center of the East Bay

Primary county resource for individuals with developmental disabilities including autism.

Why it's useful: RCEB is the gateway to services for individuals with developmental disabilities in Contra Costa and Alameda counties. They provide case management and fund services that schools don't cover, such as respite for families, behavioral services, day programs, and supported living. Getting connected early (especially for early intervention 0-3) can make a huge difference in outcomes. For school-age students, RCEB can supplement school services and help with transition planning starting at age 14.
What makes it evidence-based/trustworthy: Regional Centers operate under California's Lanterman Developmental Disabilities Services Act, which established the right to services for people with developmental disabilities. RCEB is a private nonprofit contracted by the California Department of Developmental Services and must follow state regulations. Service coordinators help families navigate systems and connect with evidence-based services. All vendors funded by RCEB must be certified by the state and provide services that meet established standards. RCEB is required to use person-centered planning approaches shown to improve quality of life outcomes.
Specific features/tools available:
  • Intake and eligibility assessment (free)
  • Early Start program for children 0-3 with developmental delays
  • Service coordination/case management
  • Individual Program Plans (IPPs) using person-centered planning
  • Funding for behavioral services (ABA therapy, behavior consultation)
  • Respite care for families (in-home and out-of-home)
  • Day programs and community integration services
  • Supported employment services
  • Independent living support and training
  • Family support services including parent training
  • Crisis intervention and emergency services
  • Assistive technology funding
  • Home modifications for accessibility
  • Transportation assistance
  • Social and recreational programs
  • Transition planning starting at age 14
  • Self-determination program for adults
